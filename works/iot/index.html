IoT Device Management

A project that designed a dashboard and an onboarding experience to enhance users confidence and improve usability based on qualitative and quantitative research.

·   ·   ·
Overview

IoT Device Management is a cloud service that updates devices' firmware and software over-the-air (OTA) and remotely monitors or controls devices (MDM).

I was the sole designer in the team who is responsible for user research, UX/UI design, and some front-end development.

Business Goal: Expanding Users

Our existing users are internal project managers and sales representives who help customers do the service for free. However, our company wants to promote the cloud service to our customers and generate revenue from it. In the future, customers can buy and operate the system themselves.

Business Goal: Expanding Users

The Challenge

Since the platform is an operating system, we emphasize usability. Therefore, we use a system usability scale (SUS) as a quick, reliable measurement tool to do the user testing with 10 general users. The report indicated that general users believed they lack sufficient background knowledge to use the system.

System Usability Scale (SUS) Results
·   ·   ·

Research Findings

Looking deeper into the general user's sentiment on the product, I discovered some insights and pain points.

1. Users with different familiarity levels have different needs

(1) Unfamiliar users need a basic background
Since the topic or content is unfamiliar to general users, they need to grasp the purpose of the cloud service or the meaning of a specific feature.

(2) Familiar users need a guide
For users with basic knowledge of OTA or MDM, they reported that they need clear indicators to use the feature.

It appears to be intuitive, but I lack a basic understanding of what this interface is exactly.

— Unfamiliar user from user testing

Even with a knowledge of geofencing, the navigation wasn't always clear. It has a clean design, but it took awhile to find exactly where it is because there weren't very clear indications.

— Familiar user from user testing

2. Technology words are hard but helpful

Users are often confused about the feature name or the technology word. However, when they are executing a mission, they often regard the technology word as keywords to navigate in our cloud service.

Technology words are hard but helpful.
3. Users share a similar hierarchy of information needs

Users first want to know what the feature is, followed by how the feature works, and followed by why they should use the feature. An example will help.

Users share a similar hierarchy of information needs.
·   ·   ·

Define the Problem

Because the platform is mainly for professional and experienced users, general users believe they lack the knowledge and confidence to use it.

How might we provide a basic understanding to better engage users?

How might we guide users to complete the task efficiently and confidently?

·   ·   ·

Develop Design Principles

With these findings in mind, we held a design sprint with stakeholders. To meet our high-level design goals, we decided on a design that is:

- Unified: Is suitable for all topics
- Guiding: Walks users through the whole process
- Welcoming: Invites users to interface in a professional manner

Developing Design Principles (Photo by Leon on Unsplash)
·   ·   ·

Generate and Prioritize Ideas

I invited and led a diverse group including managers and engineers for an online meeting that included:

(1) Brainstorming

People in various positions and departments presented different ideas.

(2) Organizing & Categorizing the Solution

Since some descriptions were repeated, I organized them into three categories—indicators, places, and support. I invited everyone to vote for the five decisions they liked best.

(3) Prioritizing with an Impact & Effort Matrix

In the Low-Hanging Fruit quadrant, we will create a prototype to compare and evaluate the result. We will ask the PM to evaluate the efforts and resources for items in the Big Bets quadrant. We will consider adding items in the Worth Considering quadrant since they take little effort.

Results

A Huge Enhancement on Usability

I was pleasantly surprised to learn that the SUS score has risen 30.6%. I believe this is mainly because of the dashboard design. However, this was only a design prototype; how the engineer implements will also be important.

Future Proposals

(1) More iterations on helping users have a basic background

Although the SUS score has risen 30.6%, participants still think that they need to learn a lot of things before they get going with the system. It's necessary to evaluate the ideas and offer the service in big bets areas which are operation videos, education training, help docs, and help chatbox.

More iterations on helping users have a basic background.
(2) Dashboard for heavy users

This project is mainly for customers who have not used the system yet. However, after using the system for a while, heavy users will need a clear information dashboard including online devices or safety warnings. We should research what information is important for heavy users first.

Dashboard for heavy users

Project Learnings

(1) Only iterative design can come close to being perfect

Although I did a lot of preparation for the design and proposed the best possible version, many problems still occurred during and/or after developing the whole website. In every iteration, I have learned something, and I can think about the bigger picture and perform better next time.

(2) Ultimate soft skills to engage stakeholders

It is important to know the critical needs of stakeholders and respond to them. For example, engineers focus on what needs modification, so I listed all the changes that needed to be made by engineers. On occasion, I will also share some interesting UX insights with them so that we can grow together.
