IoT Device Management

Research-driven redesign of onboarding and dashboard—boosted usability by 30.6% and cut task time from 18.9s to 5.2s.

·   ·   ·
Overview

Our company wanted to redesign and turned this internal software tool into a real product and release it to our customers.

In this project, we defined the customers, did usability testing, and surprisingly found that our customers think they needed to learn a lot of background knowledge or procedure before they could get going with the system.

After a series of design processes, the overall usability has increased over 30% by designing dashboard and onboarding experience.

Background

Our company is a global hardware manufacturer who sells numerous rugged tablets to countries in America, Europe, and the Middle East. The IoT Device Management is our internal software solution that remotely manages connected devices over-the-air(OTA) and provides centralized management features.

Our company wanted to redesign and turn this internal tool into a real product in order to deliver the best software impression and experience to our customers. However, we found that customers believed they needed to learn a lot of things before they could get going with the system.

Real product's screen record video

Our Team & My Role

Typically, I worked closely with PM and engineers. I led the end-to-end product design, from defining the problem to delivering the final visual artifact. In addition to this IoT device management, I was also responsible for Android and Windows applications on our tablets.

What did I do?
(1) USER RESEARCH

Framed research questions, delivered a user survey, and analyzed quantitative and qualitative data to locate pain points, gather insights, and validate the design assumptions.

(2) UX DESIGN

Designed wireframes, user flows, user journeys, and UI interfaces; hosted a design sprint; and confirmed feasibility with other stakeholders.

(3) FRONT-END DEVELOPMENT

Utilized Vuetify, a material design component framework for Vue.js, to build UI components. Additionally, used Git to sync with developers.

Part I

Business Goal: Release MVP to the Market

Our company wants to promote our cloud service as the MVP in the market, ultimately striving to achieve product-market fit (PMF).

However, we have a new target user base. The current users are internal project managers and sales representatives who are helping customers do the service for free. In the future, the user base will be external customers who will need to buy and operate the system themselves.

Part II

Improve Usability for Target Users

The Challenge

To evaluate the overall usability experience for general users, we asked 10 general users to execute three tasks and use a system usability scale (SUS) as a quick and reliable measurement tool.

The report indicated that general users believed they needed to learn a lot of things before they could get going with the system. 39% of participants navigated outside the expected path and 10% of participants left or gave up on the mission. The high percentage may suggest real-time frustration or lost users.

System Usability Scale (SUS) results
·   ·   ·

Research Findings

Looking deeper into the general user's sentiment on the product, I discovered some insights:

1. Users need a basic background and a clear indicator

For users who were not familiar with our topic or content, they needed to first grasp the purpose of the cloud service or the meaning of a specific feature before using the service; on the other hand, for users who had a basic knowledge of OTA or MDM, they reported that they instead needed clear indicators to use the feature.

It appears to be intuitive, but I lack a basic understanding of what this interface is exactly.

— Unfamiliar user from user testing

Even with a knowledge of geofencing, the navigation wasn't always clear. It has a clean design, but it took awhile to find exactly where it is because there weren't very clear indications.

— Familiar user from user testing

2. Technical terms are confusing yet helpful

Users were often confused by features’ names or technology terms. However, when they were executing a mission, they often regarded the technology terms as keywords by which to navigate in our cloud service.

Technology words are hard but helpful
3. Users share a similar hierarchy of information needs

Users first want to know what a feature is, then how the feature works, and finally, why they should use the feature. Providing examples on how to use features will definitely help users navigate the system.

Users share a similar hierarchy of information needs
·   ·   ·

Define the Problem

Because the platform is mainly for professional and experienced users, general users believe they lack the knowledge and confidence to use it.

How might we provide a basic understanding to better engage users?

How might we guide users to complete the task efficiently and confidently?

·   ·   ·

Develop Design Principles

With these findings in mind, we held a design sprint with stakeholders. To meet our high-level design goals, we decided on a design that is:

- Unified: Is suitable for all topics
- Guiding: Walks users through the whole process
- Welcoming: Invites users to interface in a professional manner

Results

(1) A huge enhancement on usability

The overall SUS score has risen 30.6%. The misclick rate has decreased from 39% to 4.6%. Moreover, the average bounce rate has decreased from 10% to 3.3%. The usability of the new version is greatly improved.

System Usability Scale (SUS) results: before & after

Design and Navigations are good.

— Comment from user testing

Interesting platform and nice user interface.

— Comment from user testing

The UI looks very clean and straightforward/intuitive.

— Comment from user testing

(2) A dashboard really helps

In the previous design, we used the device index page as the front page; participants spent 18.9 seconds finding a specific feature. However, with the new dashboard, participants only spend 5.2 seconds with no misclicks and no bounces.

Part III

Iteration & Conclusion

Future Proposals

(1) More iterations on helping users have a basic background

Although the SUS score has risen 30.6%, participants still think that they need to learn a lot of things before they get going with the system. It's necessary to evaluate the ideas and offer the service in big bets areas which are operation videos, education training, help docs, and help chatbox.

More iterations on helping users have a basic background
(2) A dashboard for returning users

This project is mainly for customers who are new to the system. However, after using the system for a while, returning users will need a clear informational dashboard that includes a list of online devices or safety warnings. We should prioritize researching what information is important for heavy users.

A dashboard for heavy users
·   ·   ·

Project Learnings

(1) Only an iterative design can come close to being perfect

Although I did a lot of preparation for the design and proposed the best possible version, many problems still occurred during and/or after developing the whole cloud service. In every iteration, I have learned something, and I continue to think about the bigger picture and make plans to perform better in the future.

(2) Developing UX is not easy in a B2B company

In a B2B business, the decision maker and the user are different. The decision maker is very specific; however, the users may be very different depending on their own situations. Therefore, it’s hard to find the perfect tester who can prove that the design really helps the business.
