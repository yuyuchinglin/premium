The Challenge

This platform is designed for professional use. Our existing internal users are project managers (PMs) and salespeople who help customers update their software and firmware. However, our company regards the cloud service as a software solution and wants to promote it to our customers. In the future, they can buy and operate the system themselves.

The Challenge: Expanding Users

Early Research Findings

Since the platform is an operating system, we emphasize usability. Therefore, we use a system usability scale (SUS) as a quick, reliable measurement tool. Our previous user testing indicated that general users believed they lack sufficient background knowledge to use the system.

System Usability Scale (SUS) Results
·   ·   ·

Emphasize Users

Looking into general user sentiment on the product, I discovered that users with different familiarity levels have different needs.

1. Unfamiliar users need a basic understanding.

Since the topic or content is unfamiliar to general users, they need to grasp the purpose of the cloud service or the meaning of a geofence.

2. Familiar users need a guide.

Users with basic knowledge of OTA or MDM reported that they could not readily find the feature and that it lacked clear indicators.

It appears to be intuitive, but I lack a basic understanding of what this interface is exactly.

— Unfamiliar user from user testing

Even with a knowledge of geofencing, the navigation wasn't always clear. It has a clean design, but it took awhile to find exactly where it is because there weren't very clear indications.

— Familiar user from user testing

·   ·   ·

Define the Problem

Because the platform is mainly for professional and experienced users, general users believe they lack the knowledge and confidence to use it.

How might we provide a basic understanding to better engage users?

How might we guide users to efficiently and confidently complete the task?

·   ·   ·

Develop Design Principles

With these findings in mind, we held a design sprint with stakeholders. To meet our high-level design goals, we decided on a design that is:

- Unified: Is suitable for all topics
- Guiding: Walks users through the whole process
- Welcoming: Invites users to interface in a professional manner

Developing Design Principles (Photo by Leon on Unsplash)
·   ·   ·

Generate and Prioritize Ideas

I invited and led a diverse group including managers and engineers for an online meeting that included:

(1) Brainstorming

People in various positions and departments presented different ideas.

(2) Organizing & Categorizing the Solution

Since some descriptions were repeated, I organized them into three categories—indicators, places, and support. I invited everyone to vote for the five decisions they liked best.

(3) Prioritizing with an Impact & Effort Matrix

In the Low-Hanging Fruit quadrant, we will create a prototype to compare and evaluate the result. We will ask the PM to evaluate the efforts and resources for items in the Big Bets quadrant. We will consider adding items in the Worth Considering quadrant since they take little effort.

Project Learnings

1) Only iterative design can come close to being perfect

Although I did a lot of preparation for the design and proposed the best possible version, many problems still occurred during and/or after developing the whole website. In every iteration, I have learned something, and I can think about the bigger picture and do better next time.

2) Ultimate soft skills to engage stakeholders

It is important to determine the critical needs of stakeholders and respond to them. For example, engineers often prefer to focus on what needs modification, so I listed all the changes that needed to be made by engineers. On occasion, I will also share some interesting UX insights with them so that we can grow together.
